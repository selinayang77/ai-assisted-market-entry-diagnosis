Intended Meaning vs. Market Interpretation

The diagnosis came from comparing what the brand wanted to communicate with what consumers actually understood.

Intended Brand Meaning

The brand intended to express a premium, feminine, natural, artistic, agave-rooted identity. Its original positioning emphasized Oaxaca origin, agave craft, ritual, natural beauty, cultural depth, and design-object aesthetics.

Market Signals

At the booth, visitors noticed the product and surfaced basic category, origin, usage, differentiation, and purchase-path questions:

  • "Is this perfume?"
  • "What is the difference between Mezcal and Tequila?"
  • "How do you drink it?"
  • "Where is Oaxaca?"
  • "What makes this product stand out?"
  • "Where can I buy it?"

Diagnostic Reframe

The diagnosis centered on what happened after visibility: visitors noticed the brand; the next challenge was turning that attention into understanding, trust, and action. The bottleneck was interpretation, education, and follow-up.

Attention was present; interpretation was missing.
Implication: Educate category first Translate craft into product value Reduce follow-up friction

The task was to translate aesthetic interest into category understanding, product value, and follow-up action.

Visitor interest stalled at three conversion bottlenecks.

Visibility to Interpretation

Main point: Visitors showed aesthetic interest and needed basic category context.

Evidence base: Repeated questions included "Is this perfume?" and "Mezcal vs. tequila?"

Feature to Value

Main point: Origin, craft, and product features were noticed; their value needed clearer translation.

Evidence base: Repeated questions included "Where is Oaxaca?" and "What makes this product stand out?"

Purchase Intent to Conversion

Main point: Some visitors showed interest; the next step after interest needed clearer direction.

Evidence base: Questions included "Where can I buy it?" and follow-up uncertainty after scanning / following.
