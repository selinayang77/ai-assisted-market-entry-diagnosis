What the Sales Data Could—and Could Not—Tell Us

The transaction records confirmed that the activation generated real tasting and purchase behavior, weakening a pure "no interest" explanation. Product-level patterns also indicated which formats attracted stronger immediate demand. However, sales data alone could not explain why visitors remained uncertain about category, origin, usage, or follow-up. The final diagnosis therefore combined transaction signals with qualitative booth evidence.

Executive Recommendation

Make mezcal understandable before asking consumers to buy.

Prioritize category education before brand persuasion: use familiar entry cues, clarify product value, and make the next action after interest frictionless.

1

Category onboarding

Explain mezcal, tequila, agave, smoky flavor, and drinking ritual before persuasion.

2

Product-value communication

Use localized opening messages and comparative tasting cues to make origin, craft, and flavor meaningful to unfamiliar consumers.

3

One-step follow-up path

Use one QR-led path to direct interested visitors toward a clear next step: learn more, follow the brand, make an inquiry, book a tasting, or purchase.

Intended Meaning vs. Market Interpretation

The diagnosis came from comparing what the brand wanted to communicate with what consumers actually understood.

Intended Brand Meaning

The brand intended to express a premium, feminine, natural, artistic, agave-rooted identity. Its original positioning emphasized Oaxaca origin, agave craft, ritual, natural beauty, cultural depth, and design-object aesthetics.

Market Signals

At the booth, visitors noticed the product and surfaced basic category, origin, usage, differentiation, and purchase-path questions:

  • "Is this perfume?"
  • "What is the difference between Mezcal and Tequila?"
  • "How do you drink it?"
  • "Where is Oaxaca?"
  • "What makes this product stand out?"
  • "Where can I buy it?"

Diagnostic Reframe

The diagnosis centered on what happened after visibility: visitors noticed the brand; the next challenge was turning that attention into understanding, trust, and action. The bottleneck was interpretation, education, and follow-up.

Attention outpaced understanding.
Implication: Educate category first Communicate product value Reduce follow-up friction

The task was to translate aesthetic interest into category understanding, product value, and follow-up action.

Competing hypotheses tested against booth evidence.

The diagnosis was selected by comparing plausible explanations, then testing each against available evidence and counter-evidence.

Hypothesis Supporting evidence Counter-evidence / limit Conclusion
Brand awareness problem The brand was a new entrant; consumers had limited prior understanding of the brand. Product design attracted attention; visitors approached the booth, asked questions, and showed purchase or tasting behavior. Awareness alone did not explain the observed friction.
Category education problem Repeated questions included "Mezcal vs. tequila?", "Where is Oaxaca?", and "How do you drink it?" Some visitors still proceeded to tasting or purchase despite limited category understanding, suggesting that the friction did not block every conversion. Strongly supported as a major friction point, though it did not prevent every conversion.
Follow-up conversion problem Questions included "Where can I buy it?"; scanning or following did not consistently create a clear next step. No complete post-event funnel data was available to quantify how many interested visitors dropped off after scanning, following, or asking where to buy. Directionally supported, but requiring next-cycle funnel measurement.
Final diagnosis: The brand did not face a pure attention problem. The larger bottleneck was converting attention into category understanding, product value, and next-step action.

The key diagnostic move was separating attention from conversion. Visitors noticed the product; the analysis focused on how attention could become category understanding, product value, and next-step action.

AI generated alternatives; evidence determined the diagnosis.

In developing this retrospective case, I used AI to generate and challenge competing explanations; the diagnosis was selected through evidence review.

Generate competing explanations → ask for falsification evidence → map available evidence → make human diagnosis choice → audit recommendations

Step 1

Generate competing explanations

The retrospective analysis considered awareness, category confusion, positioning mismatch, price resistance, and conversion friction.

Step 2

Ask for falsification evidence

The prompt asked what evidence would weaken each explanation, rather than asking the model to choose the correct answer.

Step 3

Map available evidence

Visitor questions, product-level sales patterns, booth observations, and brand positioning materials were aligned to each hypothesis.

Step 4

Make the human diagnosis choice

Explanations with limited supporting evidence were deprioritized; category education and follow-up conversion were better supported.

Step 5

Audit recommendations

Each recommendation had to trace back to a specific consumer friction point observed during or after the activation.

Candidate Actions Screened Against the Evidence

I screened AI-generated and independently considered options against the available evidence. Actions with weak links to the observed friction were deprioritized.

More social media exposure

Useful later, but it did not directly address category comprehension at the booth.

KOL activation

Insufficient evidence that influence, rather than understanding, was the main bottleneck.

Price discounting

No sufficient evidence showed price as the primary source of friction.

Broader promotions

Promotion would not clarify mezcal, tequila, Oaxaca, or drinking ritual questions.

Reinforcing feminine brand positioning

Aesthetic appeal was already creating attention; the larger task was translating category and value.

Generic awareness campaign

Awareness alone did not explain active booth questions and purchase or tasting behavior.

Business value framed as testable hypotheses.

The recommendations were not claimed as realized results. They were framed as measurable next-cycle tests.

Recommendation Expected value Test metric
Category onboarding Improve category understanding Share of visitors correctly explaining mezcal-tequila difference
Product-value communication Improve differentiation and choice confidence Message recall; tasting-to-purchase conversion
One-step follow-up path Reduce follow-up friction QR scans converting to follow, inquiry, tasting booking, or purchase

Limitations

This was a retrospective diagnosis based on one festival activation. Visitor questions and field observations were not collected through a standardized research protocol, and no complete post-event funnel data was available. The recommendations therefore represent evidence-informed hypotheses for the next activation, rather than validated causal conclusions.
